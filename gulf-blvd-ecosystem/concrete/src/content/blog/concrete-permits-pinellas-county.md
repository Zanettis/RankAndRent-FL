Permitting for concrete work in Pinellas County follows Florida Building Code requirements, with some additional overlay from individual municipalities (Indian Rocks Beach, Madeira Beach, St. Pete Beach, Clearwater, etc.). Here's what requires permits, what the process looks like, and why cutting corners on permits creates problems down the line.

## What Typically Requires a Permit in Pinellas County

**Generally requires a permit:**
- New driveway installation or full replacement
- New pool deck installation (not resurfacing an existing deck)
- Concrete patio additions larger than certain thresholds
- Concrete foundations and footings (all structural work)
- Pool cage footings
- Retaining walls above a certain height (typically 4 feet)
- Concrete for new additions or structures

**Generally does NOT require a permit:**
- Pool deck resurfacing (overlay on existing deck)
- Crack repair and sealing
- Small repairs and patches
- Concrete sealing
- HVAC/generator equipment pads (under threshold size)

**"It depends" category:**
- Driveway apron replacement (sometimes yes, sometimes no depending on municipality)
- Concrete patios (size thresholds vary by city)
- Walkways (depends on size and connection to structure)

Because rules vary by municipality on Gulf Blvd, we always check with the specific city before beginning any project where permit requirements are unclear. The consequences of unpermitted work are serious enough that this step is non-negotiable.

## Why Permits Matter

**Sale-related issues:** Pinellas County property records show permitted construction history. When you sell, buyers' attorneys pull permit history. Unpermitted structural work — an addition, a pool cage footing, a structural slab — can delay or derail a sale or require expensive retroactive permitting.

**Insurance claims:** After a hurricane or storm event, insurance adjusters look at permitted vs. unpermitted construction when evaluating claims. Unpermitted work may not be covered.

**Safety:** Permit inspections exist to catch errors before they're buried in concrete. A footing inspection that catches incorrectly placed anchor bolts prevents a pool cage collapse during the next storm. This is why the inspection process has value, not just bureaucratic inconvenience.

**HOA and condo association compliance:** Many Gulf Blvd properties are within HOAs that require proof of permits before approving contractor work. Our permit documentation satisfies these requirements.

## The Permit Process for Concrete Work

1. **Permit application:** We submit plans and application to the relevant municipality. For simple projects (driveway replacement), this is often straightforward. For structural work (foundations, retaining walls), engineered drawings may be required.

2. **Review period:** Simple permits are often approved in 1–5 business days. Complex structural permits may take 2–4 weeks.

3. **Inspections:** Most structural concrete projects require at least one inspection — typically before concrete is poured (to verify reinforcement, forms, and embedments). We coordinate inspection scheduling.

4. **Final approval:** After all required inspections pass, the permit is closed. We retain records for you.
