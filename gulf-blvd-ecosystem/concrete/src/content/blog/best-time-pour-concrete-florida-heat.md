Florida is a challenging environment for concrete work. The state's combination of intense heat, high humidity, and frequent afternoon thunderstorms creates conditions that require careful planning to achieve properly cured, durable concrete. Here's what Gulf Blvd homeowners should understand about timing.

## The Florida Concrete Problem

In most of the continental US, the primary concrete weather concern is freeze-thaw damage. Florida has essentially no freeze-thaw risk — temperatures rarely approach 32°F at sea level. Instead, the challenges here are:

**Excessive heat:** When ambient temperatures exceed 90°F (common May through October on Gulf Blvd), concrete sets faster than intended. Rapid setting reduces workability, makes proper finishing difficult, and can result in incomplete hydration — weaker concrete that's more prone to cracking.

**High humidity:** Paradoxically, high humidity can be both helpful (slows moisture evaporation from the surface) and problematic (makes it harder to judge when the surface is ready to finish).

**Afternoon thunderstorms:** Florida's rainy season (June–September) brings nearly daily afternoon thunderstorms. Rain on freshly poured concrete before it's set can wash the surface, dilute the mix, and cause surface dusting or weakness.

## Best Season: October Through April

The window from October through April is optimal for Gulf Blvd concrete work. Temperatures are mild (70s–80s°F), afternoon storm risk is significantly lower, and morning dew typically dries before pour time. This is our busiest season for a reason — the conditions simply produce better concrete.

November through February is the best of the best: cooler temperatures (sometimes 60s–70s°F), low humidity, minimal rain. Concrete placed in these months has the most forgiving cure conditions.

## Working in Summer

We work year-round, and summer pours are done successfully with proper precautions:

- **Pour early.** We target 6–8 AM starts for summer pours. This gives us the cooler morning temperatures before the midday peak.
- **Use set-retarding admixtures.** These slow the hydration reaction, giving our crews more time to properly finish the surface in hot conditions.
- **Monitor mix temperature.** Concrete mix temperature leaving the plant matters — we coordinate with our ready-mix supplier to ensure proper mix temperature.
- **Shade the pour.** For smaller projects, temporary shading can significantly reduce surface temperature and evaporation rate.
- **Cure with blankets or curing compound.** Preventing premature moisture loss is critical in Florida summers.

## The Thunderstorm Variable

In summer, we check radar obsessively. If afternoon thunderstorms are likely, we push start times earlier to ensure the concrete is past its initial set (typically 4–6 hours) before any rain arrives. We communicate this timing to homeowners — you may get a call the night before a pour with a start time adjustment based on the next day's forecast.

## What This Means for Your Project Schedule

If you want concrete work in June–August, don't be discouraged — we do it successfully. Just expect:
- Earlier start times (6 AM crews)
- Slightly longer cure-to-use periods (we wait an extra day before allowing traffic in summer heat)
- Possible weather-related reschedules (rare, but we won't pour into an afternoon storm)

For projects where timing flexibility exists, booking for October–April will typically get you slightly better conditions and often shorter wait times on our schedule.
