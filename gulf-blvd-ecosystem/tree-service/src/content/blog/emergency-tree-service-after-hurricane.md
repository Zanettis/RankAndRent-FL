A hurricane or tropical storm that makes a direct hit on the Gulf Blvd corridor can damage dozens of trees per block. In the immediate aftermath, what you do — and what you don't do — determines how quickly your property is safe and how smoothly your insurance claim goes.

Here's the protocol we recommend based on years of post-storm work along the Gulf Blvd corridor.

## Immediate Post-Storm Safety

**Do not exit your home until conditions are officially declared safe.** The outer bands of a hurricane can remain dangerous for hours after the eye passes. Storm surge can still be elevated, downed lines may be hidden under debris, and structurally compromised buildings and trees can fail without warning.

When you do go outside to assess:

- **Assume all downed lines are live.** A downed power line in contact with a tree makes the entire tree energized. Do not touch, do not approach within 30 feet. Call 911. Duke Energy's storm emergency line is active 24 hours.

- **Watch for unstable trees.** Trees that were partially uprooted during the storm may still be standing but are no longer structurally stable. Root plate heaving (the root ball lifted slightly from the ground) means the tree can fall. Saturated soil makes this worse — trees that "survived" a storm can fall in calm conditions 12–24 hours later as soil dries and compaction changes.

- **Watch for compromised structures.** Pool cage frames bent by a fallen tree may still be under load. Do not walk under or near them.

## Document Before You Cleanup

Before any branch gets moved, document everything:

- **Wide shots** showing the tree and the affected structure in the same frame, from multiple angles
- **Close shots** of where the tree contacts the roof, fence, cage, or vehicle
- **Ground-level shots** showing root zone damage for uprooted trees
- **Video walk-throughs** — many adjusters find video more useful than photos for understanding scope

Use your phone and let the metadata timestamp the files. Do not edit or filter the photos. "Raw" documentation is more useful to adjusters than enhanced images.

## Triage: What Gets Addressed First

After a major storm, every arborist on the Gulf Blvd corridor has a full call log. Here's how we triage:

**Priority 1 — Trees or major limbs on structures.** A fallen tree on your roof creates an active water intrusion risk and structural instability. This is same-day priority. Call immediately.

**Priority 2 — Trees blocking access drives and roads.** A blocked driveway or road prevents emergency vehicle access. We work these along with structural emergencies.

**Priority 3 — Hanging limbs over inhabited structures.** A large limb that is broken but still attached ("widow maker") can drop without warning. These need to be assessed and secured before people can safely be under them.

**Priority 4 — Trees down in open yard, no structural contact.** These are a cleanup job, not an emergency. They can wait until the structural and access work is complete.

**Priority 5 — Debris, small branches, scattered material.** This is the last thing we address.

## The Insurance Documentation You Need

Florida homeowner insurance policies vary, but most cover:

- **Tree damage to insured structures** (roof, attached garage, fence, pool cage, free-standing structures) — typically covered under the dwelling or other structures section
- **Vehicle damage from a fallen tree** — covered under your auto policy's comprehensive coverage, not the home policy
- **Tree removal** — only covered if the tree hit a structure. Removal of a fallen tree from the yard (no structural damage) is typically not covered.

Your arborist's written estimate is part of your claim documentation. Ask us for a written assessment when we respond to your call — we provide them as standard for post-storm jobs.

## Out-of-Area Contractors: What to Check

After every named storm that hits the Gulf Coast, the barrier island communities see an influx of out-of-area contractors — some legitimate, some not.

Before you sign anything or hand over a deposit:

1. **Verify Florida license** — Tree contractors should hold a Florida Registered Landscape Architect or General Contractor license, or operate under a licensed tree service. Search at [myfloridalicense.com](https://www.myfloridalicense.com).

2. **Request proof of insurance** — General liability AND workers' compensation. If a worker is injured on your property and the contractor is uninsured, you may be liable.

3. **Get a written estimate** — Never authorize work on a handshake. A written estimate specifies scope, materials, and total price.

4. **Do not pay more than 10–25% upfront** — Standard deposits for insurance work. Full payment before work starts is a red flag.

Local, established contractors have local licenses, local insurance, and local reputations to protect. They also stay to finish the job.
