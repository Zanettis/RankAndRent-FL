Pinellas County has one of the most comprehensive tree protection ordinances in Florida. If you own property on Gulf Boulevard — from Indian Rocks Beach to St. Pete Beach — understanding the permit requirements before you touch a tree can save you from fines that often exceed the cost of the tree work itself.

## Do You Need a Permit?

The short answer for most Gulf Blvd trees: **yes, probably.**

Pinellas County's Tree Protection Ordinance (Chapter 106 of the Pinellas County Land Development Code) requires permits for the removal of any tree with a trunk diameter of **4 inches DBH (diameter at breast height)** or larger. DBH is measured 4.5 feet above ground.

### Protected Species With Additional Requirements

Certain species receive heightened protection under the ordinance:

**Live Oak (*Quercus virginiana*)** — One of the most protected trees in Pinellas County. Removal requires a permit regardless of size. Large-canopy Live Oaks may require a mitigation replacement (typically 1–2 replacement trees per Live Oak removed).

**Sabal Palm (*Sabal palmetto*)** — Florida's state tree. Removal requires a permit. If you're removing a Sabal Palm to replace it with another Sabal Palm, the process is typically straightforward, but still requires paperwork.

**Champion Trees** — Trees registered in Pinellas County's Champion Tree Program (the largest specimen of each species on record) have additional protections and require a variance process for removal.

### Exemptions — When You Don't Need a Permit

Some situations are exempt from the permit requirement:

- Trees under 4 inches DBH
- Trees that present an **imminent hazard** (certified by an arborist in writing, with documentation)
- Trees damaged or killed by a **documented pest or disease** (requires ISA arborist certification)
- Fruit trees, ornamental trees, and certain landscape shrubs (species-dependent)
- Active construction clearing (after applicable development permits are issued)

Note: "My tree looks dead" or "it's leaning" does not automatically qualify as an imminent hazard exemption. The exemption requires a written certification from an ISA-certified arborist documenting the specific hazard. DIY declarations don't count.

## What the Permit Costs

Permit fees are set by the county and vary based on:

- Trunk diameter (DBH)
- Species (protected species may have higher fees)
- Number of trees

Typical ranges:
- Small trees (4–12 inches DBH): $75–$150
- Medium trees (12–24 inches DBH): $150–$250
- Large trees (24+ inches DBH): $200–$400+

Mitigation requirements (replacement planting) add cost if triggered. The county may require you to plant replacement trees valued at a formula-based amount calculated from the removed tree's appraised value.

## The Application Process

1. **Submit the application** — Available online through the Pinellas County portal or in person at the Development Review Services office. Required: property address, tree species, size, reason for removal, site plan showing tree location.

2. **Staff review** — County arborist reviews the application and may require a site visit for large or protected trees.

3. **Processing time** — Typically 5–15 business days for straightforward applications. More complex applications (large Live Oaks, mitigation required) can take 3–4 weeks.

4. **Permit issuance** — Permit is issued with specific conditions. Conditions may include timing restrictions (no removal during nesting season for certain species), mitigation requirements, and inspection requirements.

5. **Work completion and inspection** — For larger trees with mitigation requirements, a post-work inspection confirms compliance.

## Municipal Variations Along Gulf Blvd

Each city along Gulf Boulevard operates under Pinellas County's unified ordinance, but some municipalities have enacted additional local regulations:

**Indian Rocks Beach, Indian Shores, Madeira Beach, Treasure Island, St. Pete Beach** — These incorporated municipalities may have their own additional tree codes layered on top of the county ordinance. The more restrictive requirement applies.

**Unincorporated Pinellas County** — Some properties on Gulf Blvd corridor are in unincorporated areas; these fall under county code only.

Your arborist should identify the applicable jurisdiction during the estimate.

## Penalties for Unpermitted Removal

Removing a tree without a required permit in Pinellas County carries significant penalties:

- **Fines** — Up to $500 per day per violation for continued non-compliance
- **Mitigation** — Required replacement planting at a ratio higher than what would have been required with a permit (unpermitted removal mitigation can be 3:1 or higher)
- **Stop work orders** — If you're in the middle of a project, unpermitted tree removal can halt the entire project

Property owners are responsible for violations even if a contractor performed the work without a permit. The liability flows to the property owner, not the contractor.

## Why Contractors Skip Permits (And Why You Shouldn't Let Them)

Some contractors — especially lower-priced operators — skip permits to cut turnaround time and reduce apparent project cost. The permit fee (which they typically pass through to you) also comes out of their quote.

The problem: **you own the property and you bear the liability for the violation.** The county issues citations to the property owner, not the contractor. If an unpermitted removal comes to the county's attention (a neighbor complaint, a subsequent permit application that triggers a site history review, or a code enforcement sweep), the fines come to you.

Any contractor who tells you a permit isn't required for a 20-inch Live Oak is either uninformed or hoping you don't know better.
