Indian Shores homeowners have a specific screening problem that inland Florida residents don't deal with to the same degree: no-see-ums. These tiny biting midges — so small they can pass through standard 18×14 pool cage mesh — are a year-round presence in Gulf Beach communities, with peak activity at dawn and dusk in warm weather.

If you've ever sat on your screened porch in Indian Shores and gotten bitten despite having a functional screen enclosure, this is why.

## The No-See-Um Problem on Indian Shores Porches

No-see-ums (also called sand flies or biting midges, scientifically Culicoides) are approximately 1–3 millimeters long — small enough to pass through the 18×14 mesh openings used in most pool cages and screened porches. Standard screen is effective against mosquitoes, which are significantly larger. Against no-see-ums, it's essentially decorative.

The challenge with Indian Shores is that the Gulf of Mexico beach is the primary breeding habitat for these insects. They're concentrated near the shoreline and in areas with salt marsh. Indian Shores sits directly in this zone.

## The Solution: 20×20 No-See-Um Mesh

The fix is 20×20 mesh — a finer weave (20 threads per inch in each direction vs. 18×14) with openings small enough to block Culicoides. The trade-off is slightly reduced airflow — the finer weave lets less air through than standard mesh.

For most Indian Shores homeowners, this is an acceptable trade-off. The alternative is either staying inside during peak no-see-um hours or using a screened porch that doesn't actually keep the insects out.

## Porch Screening Options for Indian Shores Homes

**Full porch rescreening with 20×20 mesh:** The most complete solution. Replace all porch screen panels with no-see-um mesh. Cost for a standard screened porch (200–400 sq ft): $600–$1,500 depending on size.

## Why Fastener Inspection Matters for Indian Shores Porches

Indian Shores porch frames face the same salt air issues as pool cages. Steel screws in aluminum frames corrode in the coastal environment. A porch frame that's been in place 10+ years may have fasteners that are structurally compromised. We inspect fastener condition during every estimate visit and recommend replacement with Nylo-Tec or stainless screws when we find rust.
